Hypothetical Stress Scenario Engine

Mo Minoneshan
2026
GitHub Demo PDF Report

Overview

This project builds a scenario engine that generates statistically consistent cross-asset market scenarios conditional on a small set of user-specified shocks. Given a partition of the risk-factor vector into pinned factors (whose moves are fixed by the user) and free factors, the engine estimates the conditional distribution of the free factors and samples coherent scenarios from it.

Model

Partition the factor returns as \(X = (X_p, X_f)\), pinned and free. The engine estimates the conditional distribution \(X_f \mid X_p = x_p\) and returns its conditional mean, conditional covariance, and simulated scenarios.

0Executive Summary

The engine answers questions of the form: "If equities fall 20% and 10Y rates rise 150 bps, what is the implied move in FX, commodities, and related futures?" It uses a conditional multivariate Gaussian as a baseline, with EWMA and shrinkage covariance estimators, and optional copula and PCA extensions for heavier tails and dimensionality reduction.

1Motivation

Traditional stress tests often shock factors independently, which produces incoherent scenarios that ignore historical co-movement. A conditional approach preserves the empirical correlation structure: pinning a few factors implies a distribution over the rest that respects how markets actually move together.

2Risk Factor Universe

The factor set spans the major liquid asset classes, mapped to tradable instruments where possible.

2.1Equities

Broad equity indices and futures (e.g. S&P 500, Nasdaq 100), expressed as log returns.

2.2Rates

Government yields across the curve; shocks are expressed in basis points and converted to return space for estimation.

2.3FX

Major currency pairs against USD.

2.4Commodities

Energy and metals front-month futures.

2.5Futures Mapping

Each factor maps to a front-month contract; rolls are handled to produce a continuous return series.

3Data Pipeline

Prices are pulled, aligned on a common calendar, converted to log returns, and cleaned for missing data before covariance estimation. 

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

prices = load_prices(tickers)            # wide DataFrame
returns = np.log(prices).diff().dropna() # log returns
returns = returns.loc[:, valid_columns]  # drop sparse factors

4Baseline Model

Assume factor returns are jointly Gaussian with mean \(\mu\) and covariance \(\Sigma\). Partition into pinned \((p)\) and free \((f)\) blocks:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} X_f \\ X_p \end{bmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left( \begin{bmatrix} \mu_f \\ \mu_p \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} \Sigma_{ff} & \Sigma_{fp} \\ \Sigma_{pf} & \Sigma_{pp} \end{bmatrix} \right). $$

4.1Conditional Gaussian

The conditional law of the free factors given the pinned shock \(X_p = x_p\) is again Gaussian.

4.2Conditional Mean

$$ \mathbb{E}[X_f \mid X_p = x_p] = \mu_f + \Sigma_{fp}\Sigma_{pp}^{-1}(x_p - \mu_p). $$

4.3Conditional Covariance

$$ \operatorname{Cov}(X_f \mid X_p) = \Sigma_{ff} - \Sigma_{fp}\Sigma_{pp}^{-1}\Sigma_{pf}. $$

This is the Schur complement of \(\Sigma_{pp}\); it captures the residual uncertainty in the free factors once the pinned shocks are known.

4.4Sampling Scenarios

Scenarios are drawn from the conditional Gaussian via a Cholesky factor of the conditional covariance. 

mu_cond = mu_f + Sfp @ np.linalg.solve(Spp, x_p - mu_p)
cov_cond = Sff - Sfp @ np.linalg.solve(Spp, Spf)
L = np.linalg.cholesky(cov_cond)
Z = rng.standard_normal((n_scenarios, len(mu_cond)))
scenarios = mu_cond + Z @ L.T

5Covariance Estimation

The quality of the conditional distribution depends entirely on \(\Sigma\). Two robust estimators are supported.

5.1EWMA Covariance

Exponentially weighted covariance gives more weight to recent observations, with decay \(\lambda\):

$$ \Sigma_t = (1-\lambda)\, r_t r_t^\top + \lambda\, \Sigma_{t-1}. $$

5.2Shrinkage

Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage pulls the sample covariance toward a structured target \(F\) to reduce estimation error:

$$ \hat\Sigma = \delta F + (1-\delta)\, S, \qquad \delta \in [0,1]. $$

6Extensions

The Gaussian baseline understates joint tail moves. Two extensions address this.

6.1Copula Extension

A Student-t copula couples the marginals with heavier joint tails, so extreme co-movements appear more often than under a Gaussian copula.

6.2PCA Conditioning

Conditioning in principal-component space reduces dimensionality and stabilises the inversion of \(\Sigma_{pp}\) when factors are highly collinear.

7Implementation

The engine exposes a small API. The core entry point generates scenarios from a dictionary of pinned shocks. 

scenarios = engine.generate(
    pinned_factors={"NQ1": -0.20, "TY1": 0.015},
    n_scenarios=1000,
    method="t-copula",
)

9Results

Conditional scenarios reproduce realistic co-movements: an equity sell-off pulls credit-sensitive factors down and safe-haven factors up, consistent with historical stress episodes.

Factor Conditional mean Std (free)
S&P 500 −14.8% 3.1%
EURUSD −2.6% 1.4%
Gold +4.2% 2.0%

10Validation

Validation covers backtesting of conditional means, stress coherence checks, and correlation preservation between simulated and historical scenarios.

11Limitations

Assumptions

The Gaussian baseline assumes linear dependence and stable covariance; regime shifts and structural breaks can degrade the conditional estimates. Copula extensions mitigate but do not eliminate this.

12Future Work

  • Regime-switching covariance models
  • Nonlinear conditioning via flexible copulas
  • Factor-level liquidity and slippage overlays

AMathematical Derivations

The conditional Gaussian formulas follow from completing the square in the joint density; see any multivariate statistics reference for the full derivation of the conditional mean and the Schur-complement covariance.
